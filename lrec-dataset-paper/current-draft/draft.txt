\abstract{
We present the first parallel corpus for Adja, an under-resourced Gbe language spoken by approximately 1,000,000 people in Benin and Togo. The corpus contains 10,000 French-Adja sentence pairs, providing a foundation for machine translation research. We establish baseline results using fine-tuned NLLB and ByT5 models, achieving a chrF++ of 28 in the French→Adja direction, and up to a chrF++ of 31 in the Adja→French direction. This work represents the first public machine translation resource for Adja. It provides benchmarks for future studies on this under-resourced West African language. The dataset and fine-tuned models are publicly available at \url{[GITHUB URL]}.
\\ \newline \Keywords{Adja language, parallel corpus, machine translation, low-resource NLP, West African languages, Gbe languages}}

\section{Introduction}

Africa is home to over 2,000 languages \cite{eberhard2025ethnologue}, representing one of the world's richest areas in linguistic diversity, with around one third of the world's languages. Among these is Adja, a Gbe language spoken by approximately 1,000,000 people primarily in southern Benin and southeastern Togo \cite{ethnologueAja2025}. Despite its significant speaker population, Adja remains severely under-resourced in terms of natural language processing tools and datasets. To the best of our knowledge, there exist no publicly available parallel corpora, machine translation systems, or labeled computational resources for Adja.

The lack of language technology for Adja contributes to the digital marginalization of its speakers and limits access to information, education, and services in their native language. This digital divide affects not only individual speakers but also the preservation and vitality of the language itself in an increasingly connected world \cite{kornai2013digitallanguagedeath}. Developing computational resources for under-resourced languages is crucial for linguistic diversity, digital inclusion, and equitable access to technology. \cite{kornai2013digitallanguagedeath}

Machine translation (MT) systems, in particular, have the potential to bridge language barriers. However, the development of such systems requires parallel text corpora, which have been absent for Adja until now.

This paper makes the following contributions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We present the first French-Adja parallel corpus containing 10,000 sentence pairs, establishing a foundation for computational work on Adja
    \item We provide baseline machine translation results using three state-of-the-art neural models (NLLB-200 \cite{nllbteam2022languageleftbehindscaling}, mBART-50 \cite{liu2020multilingualdenoisingpretrainingneural}, and ByT5 \cite{xue2022byt5tokenfreefuturepretrained}), demonstrating both the utility of the corpus and the challenges inherent in extremely low-resource MT
    \item We establish evaluation benchmarks for future Adja machine translation research
    \item We release the corpus publicly under a non-commercial license to enable further academic research
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{African Language Technology Initiatives}

The movement to advance natural language processing for African languages has gained momentum in recent years through grassroots organizations and research communities. Masakhane \citep{orife2020masakhanemachinetranslation}, a pan-African research collective dedicated to strengthening NLP for African languages, has coordinated efforts across the continent to build MT systems for previously under-resourced languages \cite{wang2024afrimteafricometenhancingcomet,adelani-etal-2023-masakhanews, adelani2022masakhaner20africacentrictransfer}. Regional initiatives such as Ghana NLP \cite{ghananlp2020} and Lelapa AI \cite{lelapaai} have focused on specific language groups, producing datasets and models for languages including Twi, Yoruba, and isiZulu. 

Despite this progress, significant gaps remain. Of Africa's approximately 2,000 languages \cite{eberhard2025ethnologue}, only a small fraction have any computational resources. Many languages, including Adja, remain completely absent from these initiatives.

\subsection{Low-Resource Machine Translation}

Recent advances in neural machine translation have demonstrated the viability of building systems for extremely low-resource languages. Multilingual pre-trained models such as NLLB-200 \cite{nllbteam2022languageleftbehindscaling} and mBART \cite{liu2020multilingualdenoisingpretrainingneural} leverage cross-lingual transfer learning to improve performance on languages with limited parallel data. These models have shown promising results on African languages including Fon \cite{emezue-dossou-2020-ffr}, and Nko \cite{doumbouya2023machinetranslationnkotools}, even with training corpora of fewer than 10,000 sentence pairs.

Character-level and byte-level models such as ByT5 \cite{xue2022byt5tokenfreefuturepretrained} have proven particularly effective for morphologically rich and agglutinative languages, as they bypass the need for language-specific tokenization. This approach has strong results on languages with complex scripts and limited preprocessing resources \cite{10.1162/tacl_a_00448}.

\subsection{Parallel Corpus Creation for Low-Resource Languages}

Building parallel corpora for extremely low-resource languages presents significant challenges. Common approaches include mining web data, translating existing benchmark datasets like FLORES-200 \cite{nllbteam2022languageleftbehindscaling}, and crowdsourcing through platforms such as Tatoeba \cite{tatoeba}, and Mozilla Common Voice \cite{ardila-etal-2020-common}. While web mining can yield large-scale data, it is often unavailable for languages with minimal online presence, like Adja, a primarily spoken language. Benchmark translation projects produce high-quality data but require significant coordination and funding. Tatoeba offers a middle ground, providing community-contributed translations that, while not domain-specific, establish a baseline for MT development.

\subsection{The Gbe Language Family and Adja}

Adja belongs to the Gbe language cluster, which includes Fon, Ewe, Gen, and other closely related languages spoken across Benin, Togo, and Ghana \cite{kluge2005gbe,tompkins2002aja}. While some computational work has been initiated for Fon \cite{emezue-dossou-2020-ffr}, Adja has received no prior attention in the NLP literature. To the best of our knowledge, this work represents the first published computational resource for the Adja language.

\section{Corpus Creation}
\label{sec:corpus}

\subsection{Data Collection}

\textbf{Source Selection.} We selected 10,000 French sentences from Tatoeba~\cite{tatoeba}\footnote{https://tatoeba.org}, a collaborative platform for collecting translated sentences contributed by people worldwide. Sentences were selected through uniform random sampling from the full set of available French-language entries on the platform, without filtering by topic, length, or domain. While Tatoeba provides diverse sentence structures and vocabulary, the sentences are not domain-specific, there is no quality control on the submitted sentences, limitations we address in Section~\ref{sec:conclusion}.

\textbf{Translation Process.} We assembled a team of five native Adja speakers in the Couffo region of Benin, a region where Adja is predominantly spoken. The team included two translators, a government-accredited Adja language instructor and an experienced fluent speaker, and three transcribers, all native Adja speakers. 

The translation process proceeded as follows over a six-month period:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item French sentences were read aloud and their meanings discussed to ensure accurate comprehension
    \item Translators produced Adja translations orally, maintaining natural language use
    \item Transcribers recorded the Adja translations in writing
    \item Translations were typed and formatted for computational use
\end{enumerate}

This collaborative, manual process ensured high translation quality through direct involvement of native speakers with strong literacy in both French and Adja. While labor-intensive, this approach was necessitated by limited internet connectivity and varying levels of digital literacy in the region, common challenges in low-resource language documentation. Future work could explore digital tools to streamline data collection, though infrastructure barriers remain significant.

\textbf{Data Processing and Quality Control.} Following digitization, sentence pairs were processed through a dedicated cleaning pipeline to standardize encoding, normalize spacing, and ensure punctuation consistency between the French source and Adja target. The pipeline applied the following steps in order:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Unicode normalization (NFKC).} All text was normalized to NFKC form, resolving encoding inconsistencies such as composed vs.\ decomposed diacritics that arise when typing Adja characters on different keyboards.
    \item \textbf{Spacing normalization.} Double spaces were collapsed; spaces before sentence-final punctuation (\texttt{.} \texttt{,} \texttt{!} \texttt{?} \texttt{;} \texttt{:}) were removed.
    \item \textbf{Non-standard character normalization.} The Latin retroflex click \texttt{ǃ} (Unicode U+01C3), occasionally introduced by keyboards as a visually similar substitute, was replaced with standard ASCII \texttt{!}.
    \item \textbf{Bidirectional punctuation consistency.} For each pair, sentence-final punctuation was checked in both directions: if the French sentence ends with \texttt{?} or \texttt{!}, the Adja sentence is required to match; mismatches detectable from interrogative function words are corrected automatically, and ambiguous cases are flagged for human review.
    \item \textbf{Quotation mark matching.} If the French sentence contains guillemets (\texttt{«~»}) or standard double quotes, matching quotation marks are added to the Adja sentence.
\end{enumerate}

The use of two independent translators working from the same source material provided implicit quality validation, as both translators shared a common linguistic baseline and produced consistent translations. The cleaned corpus is referred to as the v2-normalized dataset and is used in all experiments reported in this paper.

\subsection{Data Splits}

We create two complementary splits from the same 10,000 sentence pairs.

\textbf{Random split.} Pairs are shuffled uniformly at random (seed 42) and sliced 80/10/10, yielding 8,000 train / 1,000 dev / 1,000 test pairs.

\textbf{Length-stratified split.} To ensure train, dev, and test sets share the same sentence-length distribution, we first bin all pairs by the length of the French source sentence: \textit{short} ($\leq$8 words), \textit{medium} (9--16 words), and \textit{long} ($>$16 words). We then sample 80/10/10 proportionally within each bin and concatenate the results, yielding 7,999 train / 999 dev / 1,002 test pairs. Table~\ref{tab:splits} reports the resulting distributions.

The statistical analysis reveals four key properties of the corpus:

\noindent\textbf{Balanced sentence length distribution.}
Both languages demonstrate median sentence lengths of 6 words with
moderate standard deviations (French: $\sigma=2.92$, Adja: $\sigma=3.18$),
indicating a well-balanced mix of sentence complexities. The corpus
comprises approximately 39\% short sentences ($\leq$5 words), 59\%
medium-length sentences (6--15 words), and 1.5\% long sentences
($>$15 words). This distribution suggests exposure to a variety of syntactic structures, from simple clausal constructions in short sentences to more complex sentences with subordination and modification, while avoiding the data sparsity issues associated with very long sentences in low-resource contexts.

\noindent\textbf{Lexical richness and morphological complexity.}
Adja demonstrates notably higher lexical diversity than French, with
a Type-Token Ratio of 0.188 compared to French's 0.172 (9.5\% higher).
This difference, combined with Adja's larger vocabulary size (12,560
vs 11,385 unique tokens despite comparable token counts), reflects
the morphological richness characteristic of Gbe languages. The high
HAPAX counts: 66\% for Adja and 63\% for French are typical of
low-resource corpora and suggest opportunities for future expansion.

\noindent\textbf{Comparable corpus sizes.}
The near-identical total token counts (French: 66,245 tokens; Adja:
66,661 tokens) ensure balanced bidirectional training, preventing
the model bias that can arise from asymmetric parallel corpora.

\noindent\textbf{Adequate vocabulary coverage.}
With over 11,000 unique tokens per language, the corpus captures
substantial lexical variation suitable for initial neural MT experiments,
though the high proportion of singleton words indicates potential
benefits from additional parallel data.

\section{Baseline Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Models}

We establish baseline results using three state-of-the-art approaches:

\textbf{NLLB-200} \cite{nllbteam2022languageleftbehindscaling}: We fine-tune the \texttt{nllb-200-distilled-600M} model, initialized from the Ewe (\texttt{ewe\_Latn}) language token as a proxy for Adja, leveraging cross-lingual transfer from the closest available Gbe language. We apply early stopping on chrF (patience 10 evaluations) to prevent overfitting.

\textbf{mBART-50} \cite{liu2020multilingualdenoisingpretrainingneural}: We fine-tune \texttt{mbart-large-50-many-to-many-mmt}, initializing from the French (\texttt{fr\_XX}) source-language embedding as a starting point for the Adja target. We register a new \texttt{aj\_Latn} language token and resize the token embeddings accordingly.

\textbf{ByT5} \cite{xue2022byt5tokenfreefuturepretrained}: We fine-tune \texttt{byt5-base}, a byte-level model that requires no language-specific tokenization, making it well-suited for a language with limited preprocessing resources.

All three models are optimized with Adafactor (learning rate $1\times10^{-4}$, no relative step), batch size 16, and early stopping on validation chrF with patience 10. We train for up to 50 epochs per model. We run each configuration with 5 random seeds (42, 123, 456, 789, 1024) and report mean $\pm$ standard deviation. Results on both the random and length-stratified splits are reported. Evaluation uses BLEU \cite{papineni2002bleu} and chrF++ \cite{popovic-2017-chrf}.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have presented the first publicly available parallel corpus and machine translation baselines for Adja, an under-resourced West African language. Our 10,000-sentence corpus, collected through a six-month collaborative effort with native Adja speakers in Benin, provides a foundation for future research on Adja language technologies.

\subsection{Limitations and Challenges}

While this corpus represents an important first step, several limitations must be acknowledged:

\textbf{Potential Data Overlap.} NLLB-200 was pre-trained on a large multilingual corpus that might includes Tatoeba data for many language pairs. However, since no Adja-language data existed in any public corpus at the time of NLLB's training, our Adja test references are not present in NLLB's training data. The French source sentences may appear in NLLB pre-training data (in French--English or other pairs), but this does not constitute target-side contamination for the French→Adja evaluation direction. ByT5 training dataset similarly contains no Adja data. We therefore consider the risk of meaningful data contamination to be negligible.

\textbf{Source Data Quality.} Our French sentences originate from Tatoeba, a crowdsourced platform where any user can contribute translations. Tatoeba does not verify whether contributors are native speakers, nor does it enforce quality control measures beyond community reporting. Consequently, the source French sentences may contain grammatical errors, unnatural phrasing, or non-standard usage that could propagate into the Adja translations.

\textbf{Lack of Domain Specificity.} The Tatoeba sentences cover random topics without thematic coherence, jumping from one subject to another without domain focus. This randomness limits the corpus's utility for domain-specific applications such as healthcare, education, or government services: contexts where machine translation would be most valuable to Adja speakers.

\textbf{Dialectal and Regional Variation.} The French sentences reflect global French usage rather than the specific variety of French spoken in Benin and Togo. Regional expressions, cultural references, and locally relevant vocabulary may be underrepresented. Similarly, while our translators were based in the Kouffo region, Adja itself may have dialectal variation across different communities that our corpus does not capture.

\textbf{Sentence-Level Scope.} Our corpus consists of isolated sentences rather than connected discourse. This limits its applicability for tasks requiring discourse-level understanding, such as document translation, conversational systems, or context-dependent interpretation.

\textbf{Corpus Size.} While 10,000 sentences represents a significant achievement for a previously undocumented language, it remains small compared to the millions of sentence pairs typically used to train high-quality MT systems. The low BLEU scores we observe (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}) reflect this data scarcity.

\textbf{Translation Direction.} Since our corpus was produced by translating French sources into Adja, future work collecting naturally produced Adja text and translating it into French would complement this resource and enable evaluation on both original-language directions.

Despite these limitations, this corpus establishes a critical baseline. The challenges we identify point toward directions for future improvement rather than undermining the value of the current resource.

\subsection{Future Directions}

Several avenues exist for extending and improving this work:

\textbf{Domain-Specific Corpora.} We plan to develop targeted corpora in high-priority domains such as healthcare, agriculture, education, and government services. These domain-specific datasets would better serve practical translation needs in Benin and Togo.


\textbf{Need for ASR development} Adja is primarily a spoken language, and the most impactful language technologies for its speakers would likely be speech-based. The oral translation process we used—in which translators produced Adja translations aloud before transcription was designed in part to preserve natural spoken Adja. Future work could leverage audio to develop an automatic speech recognition (ASR) corpus for Adja. ASR development for Adja would complement the MT work presented here, enabling end-to-end spoken language translation pipelines that could serve the many Adja speakers who communicate primarily in speech rather than writing. Machine Translation was only the first step. 

\textbf{Community Engagement.} Sustainable development of Adja language technology requires ongoing collaboration with the Adja-speaking community. We aim to establish feedback mechanisms for corpus refinement and to identify the most pressing language technology needs.

\textbf{Data Availability.} The dataset and fine-tuned models are publicly available on GitHub at \url{[GITHUB URL]} and on Hugging Face at \url{[HF URL]} under a non-commercial license to protect against exploitative use while enabling academic research.

This work represents the first step in a longer journey toward comprehensive language technology support for Adja. We hope this resource catalyzes further research and demonstrates the feasibility of building NLP tools for truly under-resourced languages.
